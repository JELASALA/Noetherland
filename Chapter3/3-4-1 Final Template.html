3.4 Isovectors of PDE Systems

3.4.1 Introduction

Before jumping with both feet into full featured Suhubi/Edelen approach we provide baby steps by showing one way to encode a PDE in EDS using something like the “Harrison/Burke process”. We will treat the ever-popular 1D heat equation. \begin{equation}{u_{xx}} = {u_t}\end{equation} First we change this second order PDE into a system of two FOPDEs by redefining variables. This is in the same spirit as the full Suhubi process. Let \begin{equation}{u_x} = w\notag\end{equation} then the PDE reads \begin{equation}{w_x} = {u_t}\notag\end{equation} Our two coupled FOPDEs are \begin{equation} \begin{split} {u_x} - w = 0\\ {u_t} - {w_x} = 0\\ \end{split} \label{e1}\end{equation} At this (early) stage Harrison (1997 - Harrison - Differential Form Symmetry Analysis of Two Equations) invokes two forms “by inspection” that when “pulled back” and “annulled” yield these coupled FOPDEs. Instead we will follow a more direct process, with a step that may seem non-obvious but it at least enables a more prescriptive process.

Multiply each FOPDE by the volume form $dx \wedge dt$ for the $n = 2$ manifold covered by $\left( {x,t} \right)$: \begin{equation}\left( {{u_x} - w} \right)dx \wedge dt = 0\notag\end{equation} \begin{equation}\left( {{u_t} - {w_x}} \right)dx \wedge dt = 0\notag\end{equation} Distribute and form total differentials of dependent variables where possible: \begin{equation}{u_x}dx \wedge dt - wdx \wedge dt = \left( {du - {u_t}dt} \right) \wedge dt - wdx \wedge dt = du \wedge dt - wdx \wedge dt = 0\notag\end{equation} \begin{equation}{u_t}dx \wedge dt - {w_x}dx \wedge dt = - \left( {du - {u_x}dx} \right) \wedge dx - \left( {dw - {w_t}dt} \right) \wedge dt = - du \wedge dx - dw \wedge dt = 0\notag\end{equation} where we have utilized the chain rule and exploited the fact that some elements will vanish from a form wedging with itself (grayed out). Rearranging some of the wedge products: \begin{equation}du \wedge dt - wdx \wedge dt = 0 \equiv {\omega ^1}\notag\end{equation}\begin{equation}dx \wedge du + dt \wedge dw = 0 \equiv {\omega ^2}\notag\end{equation} This encodes the 1D heat equation in the language of exterior differential forms; the two 2-forms form an ideal $I\left\{ {{\omega ^1},{\omega ^2}} \right\}$ (but not a closed ideal). These forms live in a higher level jet space, where all 4 variables $\left( {x,t,u,w} \right)$ are treated as independent and which, when pulled back to the base manifold, yield the original FOPDEs.

We now “solve” the EDS system by applying the requirement for isovectors using Cartan’s Magic Formula (3.3.1): \begin{equation}{\mathcal{L}_V}\left( {{\omega ^1}} \right) = \left\langle {V,d{\omega ^1}} \right\rangle + d\left\langle {V,{\omega ^1}} \right\rangle = \Lambda _\beta ^1 \wedge {\omega ^\beta }\tag{A}\label{eqA}\end{equation} \begin{equation}{\mathcal{L}_V}\left( {{\omega ^2}} \right) = \left\langle {V,d{\omega ^2}}\right\rangle + d\left\langle{V,{\omega ^2}}\right\rangle = \Lambda _\beta ^2 \wedge {\omega ^\beta }\tag{B}\label{eqB}\end{equation} with $\beta = 1,2$. Our challenge is to find vectors V that satisfy this system of equations. In the process we will find the coupling “constants” $\Lambda _\beta ^\alpha $ that make this true but otherwise have no significance. The real prize is finding the coefficients of the isovector, symbolically expressed as \begin{equation}V = T{\partial _t} + X{\partial _x} + U{\partial _u} + W{\partial _w}\notag\end{equation} where the coefficients are functions of the independent and dependent variables. Starting with \eqref{eqA} we will need \begin{equation}\left\langle{V,{\omega ^1}}\right\rangle =\left\langle {X{\partial _x} + T{\partial _t} + U{\partial _u} + W{\partial _w},du \wedge dt - wdx \wedge dt}\right\rangle = - Xwdt - Tdu + wTdx + Udt\notag\end{equation} \begin{equation}\left\langle{dV,{\omega ^1}}\right\rangle = - Xdw \wedge dt - wdX \wedge dt - dT \wedge du + wdT \wedge dx + Tdw \wedge dx + dU \wedge dt\notag\end{equation} \begin{equation}\left\langle{V,d{\omega ^1}}\right\rangle = \left\langle{X{\partial _x} + T{\partial _t} + U{\partial _u} + W{\partial _w}, - dw \wedge dx \wedge dt}\right\rangle = + Xdw \wedge dt - Tdw \wedge dx - Wdx \wedge dt\notag\end{equation} Summing \begin{equation}{\mathcal{L}_V}\left( {{\omega ^1}} \right) = - Xdw \wedge dt - wdX \wedge dt - dT \wedge du + wdT \wedge dx + Tdw \wedge dx + dU \wedge dt + Xdw \wedge dt - Tdw \wedge dx - Wdx \wedge dt\notag\end{equation}So the left hand side of equation \eqref{eqA} is a degree-2 sum of terms: \begin{equation}LHS\left( A \right) = - wdX \wedge dt - dT \wedge du + wdT \wedge dx + dU \wedge dt - Wdx \wedge dt\notag\end{equation} The right hand side can be expressed: \begin{equation}RHS\left( A \right) = \Lambda _1^1{\omega ^1} + \Lambda _2^1{\omega ^2} = \Lambda _1^1du \wedge dt - \Lambda _1^1wdx \wedge dt + \Lambda _2^1dx \wedge du + \Lambda _2^1dt \wedge dw\notag\end{equation} where the coefficients must be zero-forms (functions) to match the degree from the LHS. Repeating with equation \eqref{eqB} we find: \begin{equation}LHS\left( B \right) = dT \wedge dw + dX \wedge du - dU \wedge dx - dW \wedge dt\notag\end{equation}\begin{equation}RHS\left( B \right) = \Lambda _1^2du \wedge dt - \Lambda _1^2wdx \wedge dt + \Lambda _2^2dx \wedge du + \Lambda _2^2dt \wedge dw\notag\end{equation} The full solution entails expanding the total differentials of the isovector coefficients using the chain rule, converting them into a sum of basis forms, resulting in a set of coupled determining equations, much as the vector approach does. The full solution can be seen at Appendix 3.4A. Here we will test one of the discovered isovectors for self-consistency: \begin{equation}{V_7} = 2t{\partial _t} + x{\partial _x} + u{\partial _u}\notag\end{equation}or \begin{equation}T = 2t,\;\;X = x,\;\;U = u\notag\end{equation}so \begin{equation}dT = 2dt,\;\;dX = dx,\;\;dU = du\notag\end{equation}

Apply to equation \eqref{eqA}: \begin{equation}{\text{LHS(A)}} = - wdx \wedge dt - 2dt \wedge du + 2wdt \wedge dx + du \wedge dt = - 3wdx \wedge dt + 3du \wedge dt\notag\end{equation}

\begin{equation}RHS\left( A \right) = \Lambda _1^1du \wedge dt - \Lambda _1^1wdx \wedge dt + \Lambda _2^1dx \wedge du + \Lambda _2^1dt \wedge dw\notag\end{equation}

This is satisfied by $\Lambda _1^1 = 3\;{\text{and}}\;\Lambda _2^1 = 0$, so ${\mathcal{L}_V}\left( {{\omega ^1}} \right) = 3{\omega ^1}$. Apply to equation \eqref{eqB}:

\begin{equation}LHS\left( B \right) = 2dt \wedge dw + dx \wedge du - du \wedge dx = 2{\color{blue}dt \wedge dw} + 2{\color{blue}dx \wedge du}\notag\end{equation}

\begin{equation}RHS\left( B \right) = \Lambda _1^2{\color{blue}du \wedge dt }- \Lambda _1^2w{\color{blue}dx \wedge dt }+ \Lambda _2^2{\color{blue}dx \wedge du} + \Lambda _2^2{\color{blue}dt \wedge dw}\notag\end{equation}

This is satisfied for Thus $\Lambda _1^2 = 0\;{\text{and}}\;\Lambda _2^2 = 2$:

\begin{equation}{\mathcal{L}_V}\left( {{\omega ^2}} \right) = 2{\omega ^2}\notag\end{equation}

As you can glean from the above, the various blue highlighted form combinations are used to define the “monomials” in the determining equation process.
