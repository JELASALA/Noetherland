3.4 Isovectors of PDE Systems

3.4.4  PDE Balance Form

A visit to Appendix 3.4B should give you a sense of how complex the EDS process can be for the general PDE case. There is a special structure of PDEs called balance equations by both Suhubi and Edelen, which when a PDE is so structured enables a direct passage from the messy Lie derivative expansions demonstrated in APPENDIX 3.4B to a more or less compact, formula driven template for finding the determining equations in terms of the vector components. In the $N = 1$ case one then inserts the universal function to obtain a coupled set of determining equations for $F$. The student then applies his/her ingenuity to tease out the form of F in terms of other functions whose dependencies eventually lead to integration constants that distinguish different isovectors of the group. There is also a rather longer process for the $N > 1$ case.

In defense of this specialization he says "The reduced determining equations for isovector components are explicitly obtained for balance equations which play an important part in physical and engineering sciences … [T]his approach proves to be quite fruitful as far as the analytical procedures are concerned in balance equations derived from conservation laws. Since natural laws are generally of this form, many field equations encountered in physics and engineering fall naturally into this category. Thus, we can say that balance equations are come across most frequently in practical applications.” The basic form of balance equations of $m + 1$ order (Suhubi clarifies the apparent symbolic order redefinition on page 522) is \begin{equation}\frac{{\partial {\Sigma ^{\alpha i}}}}{{\partial {x^i}}} + {\Sigma ^\alpha } = 0\notag\end{equation} with \begin{equation}{\Sigma ^\alpha } = {\Sigma ^\alpha }\left( {{x^i},{u^\alpha },u_{,{i_1}}^\alpha , \ldots ,u_{,{i_1}{i_2} \ldots {i_m}}^\alpha } \right)\notag\end{equation} and \begin{equation}{\Sigma ^{\alpha i}} = {\Sigma ^{\alpha i}}\left( {{x^i},{u^\alpha },u_{,{i_1}}^\alpha , \ldots ,u_{,{i_1}{i_2} \ldots {i_m}}^\alpha } \right)\notag\end{equation} where at least one of the functions ${\Sigma ^{\alpha i}}\left( {{x^i},{u^\alpha },u_{,{i_1}}^\alpha , \ldots ,u_{,{i_1}{i_2} \ldots {i_m}}^\alpha } \right)$ contains an $m^{th}$ order derivative $u_{,{i_1}{i_2} \ldots {i_m}}^\alpha $. Here, as earlier, $\alpha $ runs from 1 to $N$, the number of dependent variables (he has dropped the distinction between $A$ and $N$). In terms of the auxiliary variables in the contact manifold: \begin{equation}{\Sigma ^\alpha } = {\Sigma ^\alpha }\left( {{x^i},{u^\alpha },v_{{i_1}}^\alpha , \ldots ,v_{{i_1}{i_2} \ldots {i_m}}^\alpha } \right)\notag\end{equation} and \begin{equation}{\Sigma ^{\alpha i}} = {\Sigma ^{\alpha i}}\left( {{x^i},{u^\alpha },v_{{i_1}}^\alpha , \ldots ,v_{{i_1}{i_2} \ldots {i_m}}^\alpha } \right)\notag\end{equation} We can also recast the fundamental $n$-form \begin{equation}{\omega ^a} = {F^a}\mu \notag\end{equation} as the balance $n$-form: \begin{equation}{\omega ^\alpha } = d{\Sigma ^{\alpha i}} \wedge {\mu _i} + {\Sigma ^\alpha }\mu \notag\end{equation}

\begin{equation}{\mathcal{L}_V}\left( {{\omega ^a}} \right) = \lambda _\beta ^a{\omega ^\beta } + \mathop \sum \limits_{r = 0}^{m - 1} \gamma _\beta ^{a\;{i_1} \ldots {i_r}} \wedge \sigma _{{i_1} \ldots {i_r}}^\beta  + \Gamma _\beta ^{a\;{i_1} \ldots {i_{m - 1}}} \wedge d\sigma _{{i_1} \ldots {i_{m - 1}}}^{\;\beta }\;\notag\end{equation}

and for the $N = 1$ case:

\begin{equation}{\mathcal{L}_V}\left( \omega  \right) = \lambda \omega  + \mathop \sum \limits_{r = 0}^{m - 1} {\gamma ^{\;{i_1} \ldots {i_r}}} \wedge \sigma _{{i_1} \ldots {i_r}}^\; + {\Gamma ^{{i_1} \ldots {i_{m - 1}}}} \wedge d\sigma _{{i_1} \ldots {i_{m - 1}}}^\;\label{ivN1}\end{equation}

with the Lie derivative expressed as

\begin{equation}{\mathcal{L}_V}\left( \omega  \right) = V\left( \Sigma  \right)\mu  + \left[ {dV\left( {{\Sigma ^i}} \right) + \Sigma d{X^i}} \right] \wedge {\mu _i} - d{X^j} \wedge d{\Sigma ^i} \wedge {\mu _{ji}}\notag\end{equation}

where ${\mu _{ji}}$ is “missing” $d{x^i}$ and $d{x^j}$ (when expressed explicitly one must take care of the overall sign, which depends on the location of these 1-forms in the volume one-form).
