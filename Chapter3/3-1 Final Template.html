3.1. Introduction to Differential Forms and Their Operations

Exterior Differential System (EDS) calculus is in many ways dual to the vector approach in discovering symmetries.  Indeed, every tangent vector field has a dual differential form field.  And the Lie group algebra among vector fields has a dual in EDS ideals.  Just as commutation among members of a Lie group results in vectors in the Lie group (or linear combinations), exterior products of members of an ideal with any other form in the manifold always produce other members of the ideal.  Like Lie algebra, a member of an ideal cannot escape the club by fundamental operations among members of the club.

In this brief introduction we learn the properties of differential forms and their manipulation.  It is focused around the interior product or contraction of forms with their dual partners: tangent vectors.

Differential forms can be defined in terms their action on tangent vectors, or as Olver puts it, in terms of their “evaluations on the tangent vector.” [IN INTRO USE THE PAC-MAN TO DEMONSTRATE THE RELATIONSHIP]. We can represent a 1-form (there are different degrees of forms) in the basis coordinates in which tangent vectors are represented as:

\begin{equation}\omega  = adx + bdy + cdz\notag\end{equation} in 3 dimensions, or more compactly in n-dimensions (using the Einstein summation convention):

\begin{equation}\omega  = {\omega _i}d{x^i},\;i = 1 \ldots n\notag\end{equation} where the ${\omega _i}$ are functions and the $d{x^i}$ are appropriate differential elements, or basis 1-forms.  To “evaluate” a 1-form on a vector we compute the interior product or contraction of a 1-form with a vector in that space

\begin{equation}V = {v^j}\frac{\partial }{{\partial {x^j}}}\notag\end{equation}as \begin{equation}\left\langle {\omega ,V} \right\rangle  \equiv \left\langle {V,\omega } \right\rangle  = \left\langle {{\omega _i}d{x^i},{v^j}\frac{\partial }{{\partial {x^j}}}} \right\rangle  = {\omega _i}{v^j}\delta _j^i = {\omega _i}{v^i}\notag\end{equation} \begin{equation}\left\langle {\omega ,V} \right\rangle  \equiv \left\langle {V,\omega } \right\rangle  = \left\langle {{\omega _i}d{x^i},{v^j}\frac{\partial }{{\partial {x^j}}}} \right\rangle  = {\omega _i}{v^j}\delta _j^i = {\omega _i}{v^i}\notag\end{equation} The Kroneker delta represents the “orthonormality” of the basis 1-forms and the vector components.  That is \begin{equation}\left\langle {dx,\frac{\partial }{{\partial x}}} \right\rangle  = \left\langle {dy,\frac{\partial }{{\partial y}}} \right\rangle  = \left\langle {dz,\frac{\partial }{{\partial z}}} \right\rangle  = 1\notag\end{equation} and “cross” contractions are zero.  Contraction produces a form one-degree lower than the input form.  A 1-form contracted with a vector results in a function, which may be thought of as a zero-form. The importance of the contraction operation will be evident when we discuss the Lie derivative later, in connection with isovectors.  Contractions of a vector with higher degree forms is explained further below after a discussion of how one generates forms of higher degrees.

We can create a 2-form (differential form of degree 2) as an exterior product or wedge product of two basis 1-forms:

\begin{equation}\sigma  = {\sigma _{ij}}d{x^i} \wedge d{x^j},\;i \ne j\notag\end{equation} The reason for the restriction $i \ne j$ is that the wedge product of a 1-form with itself vanishes, owing to the anti-symmetry property of the exterior product of 1-forms.  For 1-forms $\alpha \;{\text{and}}\;\beta $:

\begin{equation}\beta  \wedge \alpha  =  - \alpha  \wedge \beta \notag\end{equation} The reason for this anti-symmetry property is related to the cross-product of vectors, which gives the signed area of the parallelogram formed by two such vectors and figures into Stokes Theorem.  This is well described in Chapter 1 of Flanders [ref].  If we replace $\beta $ by $\alpha $ in the above equation we see the wedge product of $\alpha $ with itself must be zero.  The wedge product is fully defined by the following properties, based on Suhubi equations (5.2.2):

\begin{equation}\alpha  \wedge \left( {\beta  + \gamma } \right) = \alpha  \wedge \beta  + \alpha  \wedge \gamma \tag{Wedge-a}\label{Wa}\end{equation}

\begin{equation}\left( {\alpha  + \beta } \right) \wedge \gamma  = \alpha  \wedge \gamma  + \beta  \wedge \gamma \tag{Wedge-b}\label{Wb}\end{equation}

\begin{equation}\alpha  \wedge \left( {\beta  \wedge \gamma } \right) = \left( {\alpha  \wedge \beta } \right) \wedge \gamma  = \alpha  \wedge \beta  \wedge \gamma \tag{Wedge-c}\label{Wc}\end{equation}

\begin{equation}\beta  \wedge \alpha  = {\left( { - 1} \right)^{kl}}\alpha  \wedge \beta ;\;\alpha  \in {\Lambda ^k}\left( M \right);\;\beta  \in {\Lambda ^l}\left( M \right)\tag{Wedge-d}\label{Wd}\end{equation}

Now this set of relations applies to forms of any degree, indicated by the symbols $\alpha  \in {\Lambda ^k}\left( M \right)$ and $\beta  \in {\Lambda ^l}\left( M \right)$, where ${\Lambda ^k}\left( M \right)$ represents all $k$ -forms in the manifold $M$.  Note the first relation implies $\beta $ and $\gamma $ are of the same degree, since they must be to be added together.  The fourth property is a generalization of the consequence \begin{equation}\omega  \wedge \omega  = 0;\;\omega  \in {\Lambda ^1}\left( M \right)\notag\end{equation} which is true for all odd degree forms.

One might expect that wedge products of even-degree forms with themselves would not vanish, but in many cases they do, which can be seen easily by computing, say, $\omega  = dx \wedge dy$ with itself – it does indeed vanish.  For a counterexample compute the product of the following 2-form in ${E_4}$ with itself: \begin{equation}\omega  = dx \wedge dy + dz \wedge dw\notag\end{equation}

Whereas contractions reduce the degree of forms, differentiation of forms (or exterior differential operation) produces a form one degree higher (or vanishes).  The differential operation on forms obeys the following properties: \begin{equation}d\left( {\alpha  + \beta } \right) = d\alpha  + d\beta \tag{Diff-a}\label{Da}\end{equation} \begin{equation}d\left( {a\beta } \right) = a\;d\beta ,\;a \in \mathbb{R}\tag{Diff-b}\label{Db}\end{equation} \begin{equation}d\left( {\alpha  \wedge \beta } \right) = d\alpha  \wedge \beta  + {\left( { - 1} \right)^{{\text{deg}}\left( \alpha  \right)}}\alpha  \wedge d\beta \tag{Diff-c}\label{Dc}\end{equation} \begin{equation}d\left( {d\alpha } \right) = {d^2}\alpha  = 0;\;\alpha  \in \Lambda \left( M \right)\tag{Diff-d}\label{Dd}\end{equation} \begin{equation}df = {f_{,i}}\;d{x^i};\;f \in {\Lambda ^0}\left( M \right)\tag{Diff-e}\label{De}\end{equation} provided by Suhubi in Section 5.8 (and others). Thus the differential operation on forms behaves as one might expect, except for the possible need to alternate signs moving through a wedge product and that repeated operations on the same form vanish. So if $\omega  = fdx$, then

\begin{equation}d\omega  = df \wedge dx + f \wedge \left( {\color{silver}ddx} \right) = df \wedge dx\notag\end{equation} as the exterior derivative of an “exact” differential form is zero.  In ${E_3}$ for $f = f\left( {x,y,z} \right)$ we have:

\begin{equation}d\omega  = df \wedge dx = \frac{{\partial f}}{{\partial x}}{\color{silver}dx \wedge dx} + \frac{{\partial f}}{{\partial y}}dy \wedge dx + \frac{{\partial f}}{{\partial z}}dz \wedge dx =  - \frac{{\partial f}}{{\partial y}}dx \wedge dy - \frac{{\partial f}}{{\partial z}}dx \wedge dz\notag\end{equation} where we reordered the surviving terms, inserting negative signs for each reversal of 1-forms.

Returning now to contractions, we are ready to compute the interior product (contraction) of a 2-form with a vector: \begin{equation}\left\langle {\sigma ,V} \right\rangle  = \left\langle {{\sigma _{ij}}d{x^i} \wedge d{x^j},{v^k}\frac{\partial }{{\partial {x^k}}}} \right\rangle \notag\end{equation}\begin{equation} = {\sigma _{ij}}{v^k}\left\langle {d{x^i},\frac{\partial }{{\partial {x^k}}}} \right\rangle  \wedge d{x^j} - {\sigma _{ij}}{v^k}d{x^i} \wedge \left\langle {d{x^j},\frac{\partial }{{\partial {x^k}}}} \right\rangle \notag\end{equation}\begin{equation} = {\sigma _{ij}}{v^k}\delta _k^id{x^j} - {\sigma _{ij}}{v^k}\delta _k^jd{x^i} = {\sigma _{kj}}{v^k}d{x^j} - {\sigma _{ik}}{v^k}d{x^i} \equiv \alpha \notag\end{equation} Note we have used the property that $f \wedge \sigma  = f\sigma $ for $f$ a function and $\sigma $ a form.  The contraction operation produces a form one degree lower than the form contracted with the vector. The negative sign in the second term is due to the anti-symmetric nature of the wedge product, seen in the last operation in (5.2.2).  Essentially, we needed to move the $d{x^j}$ ahead of the $d{x^i}$ before contracting it with the vector operator.

The complete properties for contractions are given by Suhubi (5.4.1), where he uses the symbol ${{\text{i}}_V}\left( \omega  \right)$ for the contraction $\left\langle {\omega ,V} \right\rangle $ or equivalently $\left\langle {V,\omega } \right\rangle $, repeated here with our notation.

\begin{equation}\left\langle {\omega ,f} \right\rangle  = 0;\;\;V \in {T^0}\left( M \right);\;f \in {\Lambda ^0}\left( M \right)\tag{Con-a}\label{Ca}\end{equation}

\begin{equation}\left\langle {\omega ,V} \right\rangle  \equiv \left\langle {V,\omega } \right\rangle  \equiv \omega \left( V \right) = {\omega _i}{v^i} \in {\Lambda ^0}\left( M \right);\;\omega  \in {\Lambda ^1}\left( M \right)\tag{Con-b}\label{Cb}\end{equation}

\begin{equation}\left\langle {\alpha  + \beta ,V} \right\rangle  = \left\langle {\alpha ,V} \right\rangle  + \left\langle {\beta ,V} \right\rangle \tag{Con-c}\label{Cc}\end{equation}

\begin{equation}\left\langle {\alpha  \wedge \beta ,V} \right\rangle  = \left\langle {\alpha ,V} \right\rangle  \wedge \beta  + {\left( { - 1} \right)^{{\text{deg}}\left( \alpha  \right)}}\alpha  \wedge \left\langle {\beta ,V} \right\rangle \tag{Con-d}\label{Cd}\end{equation}

There are no surprises here; the properties of contraction follow from the properties of the wedge product.  The last expression, employed in the 2-form example above, can be motivated with an example.

Let \begin{equation}\alpha  = {a_k}d{x^k}\notag\end{equation} \begin{equation}\beta  = {b_{ij}}d{x^i} \wedge d{x^j}\notag\end{equation} \begin{equation}V = {v^l}\frac{\partial }{{\partial {x^l}}}\notag\end{equation}Compute \begin{equation}\left\langle {\alpha  \wedge \beta ,V} \right\rangle  = \left\langle {{a_k}{b_{ij}}d{x^k} \wedge d{x^i} \wedge d{x^j},{v^l}\frac{\partial }{{\partial {x^l}}}} \right\rangle\notag\end{equation} \begin{equation}\left\langle {\alpha  \wedge \beta ,V} \right\rangle  = {a_k}{b_{ij}}{v^l}\left\langle {d{x^k} \wedge d{x^i} \wedge d{x^j},\frac{\partial }{{\partial {x^l}}}} \right\rangle\notag\end{equation} \begin{equation}\left\langle {\alpha  \wedge \beta ,V} \right\rangle  = {a_k}{b_{ij}}{v^l}\left[ {\left\langle {d{x^k} \wedge d{x^i} \wedge d{x^j},\frac{\partial }{{\partial {x^l}}}} \right\rangle  - \left\langle {d{x^i} \wedge d{x^k} \wedge d{x^j},\frac{\partial }{{\partial {x^l}}}} \right\rangle  + \left\langle {d{x^j} \wedge d{x^i} \wedge d{x^k},\frac{\partial }{{\partial {x^l}}}} \right\rangle } \right]\notag\end{equation} \begin{equation}\left\langle {\alpha  \wedge \beta ,V} \right\rangle  = {a_k}{b_{ij}}{v^l}\left( {\delta _l^kd{x^i} \wedge d{x^j} - \delta _l^id{x^k} \wedge d{x^j} + \delta _l^jd{x^i} \wedge d{x^k}} \right)\notag\end{equation} \begin{equation}\left\langle {\alpha  \wedge \beta ,V} \right\rangle  = {a_k}{b_{ij}}{v^k}d{x^i} \wedge d{x^j} + {b_{ij}}{v^j}d{x^i} \wedge \alpha  - {b_{ij}}{v^i}\alpha  \wedge d{x^j}\notag\end{equation} \begin{equation}\left\langle {\alpha  \wedge \beta ,V} \right\rangle  = {a_k}{v^k}\beta  - \left( {{b_{ij}}{v^j}\alpha  \wedge d{x^i} + {b_{ij}}{v^i}\alpha  \wedge d{x^j}} \right)\notag\end{equation} \begin{equation}\left\langle {\alpha  \wedge \beta ,V} \right\rangle  = {a_k}{v^k}\beta  - \alpha  \wedge {b_{ij}}\left( {{v^j}d{x^i} + {v^i}d{x^j}} \right)\notag\end{equation} Reconstituting the contractions: \begin{equation}\left\langle {\alpha  \wedge \beta ,V} \right\rangle  = \left\langle {\alpha ,V} \right\rangle  \wedge \beta  - \alpha  \wedge \left\langle {\beta ,V} \right\rangle \;\;\color{blue}\blacksquare \notag\end{equation}

In the 2-form example above both basis forms were first degree, so carrying the second vector past the first in the wedge product to operate on it with the vector entailed using a negative sign.  Had it been a 3-form we would pick up a negative sign on the first “hop” but have a net positive sign on the second “hop.”  For example:

\begin{equation}\left\langle {\sigma ,v} \right\rangle  = \left\langle {adx \wedge bdy \wedge cdz,p\frac{\partial }{{\partial x}} + q\frac{\partial }{{\partial y}} + r\frac{\partial }{{\partial z}}} \right\rangle \notag\end{equation} \begin{equation}\color{blue} = pabc\left\langle {dx \wedge dy \wedge dz,\frac{\partial }{{\partial x}}} \right\rangle  + qabc\left\langle {dx \wedge dy \wedge dz,\frac{\partial }{{\partial y}}} \right\rangle  + rabc\left\langle {dx \wedge dy \wedge dz,\frac{\partial }{{\partial z}}} \right\rangle \notag\end{equation} \begin{equation}\color{blue} = pabc\left\langle {\frac{\partial }{{\partial x}},dx \wedge dy \wedge dz} \right\rangle  + qabc\left\langle {\frac{\partial }{{\partial y}},dx \wedge dy \wedge dz} \right\rangle  + rabc\left\langle {\frac{\partial }{{\partial z}},dx \wedge dy \wedge dz} \right\rangle \notag\end{equation} \begin{equation} = p\left( {abc} \right)dy \wedge dz - q\left( {abc} \right)dx \wedge dz + r\left( {abc} \right)dx \wedge dy\notag\end{equation} resulting in a 2-form. We added intermediate lines (in blue) to emphasize three things:  first, we can break this into three individual contractions by property \eqref{Cc}.  Second, functions (a, b, c, p, q, r) move “through” the operation by property \eqref{Cb}.  Third, we can reverse the form and vector in the contraction symbology without changing its meaning, facilitating an imaginary rightward “hopping” of the vector component over any 1-forms until we get to one that results in a non-zero evaluation.  An odd number of hops generates a negative sign.

It will serve you well to practice n-form contractions as we will be computing them heavily in the section on Isovectors in conjunction with the Lie Derivative.
