Section 1.2

The Manifold

We start with a 2D representation of a manifold:

The dots are there just to indicate the idea of "points."  The manifold includes all of the points in a continuous manner inside those blue boundaries.  Note that those boundaries might be at infinity.  And the reason we curved them was to suggest that this set might be "curved" - think of a small patch of the surface of a sphere, for instance like this:

Now the fun begins and hopefully this will show the flexibility of a manifold.  We get to pick and choose what kind of "structure" to add to OUR manifold.  Our first structure will be to add "lines" that are continuous sets of adjacent points that connect any point in the manifold with any other point:

The lines certainly do not have to be "straight," just continuous.  Now this might be unsettling.  We are NOT going to make any assumptions about distance and angle (which entails a metric) -- just lines.  This will be hard to do after having years and years (thousands actually) of Euclidean geometry pounded into our heads.  The basic reason we want to emphasize lines instead of distance and angle is that we want some of the possible lines one could imagine to be special, as you'll see in a moment.

The first set of special lines will come directly from Cartesian-Euclidean geometry -- coordinate axes.  All of our points are going to have coordinates and it's a bit hard to see how to do that without some coordinate axes.  However, we demand that each coordinate axis be defined as a map from the real numbers to the manifold coordinates, $x|\;\mathbb{R}\mapsto M\;{\text{as}}\;s\mapsto x\left( s \right)$, as parametric equations.  Here's a sequence from left to right depicting these constructions:

just to emphasize that the domains of those maps are not in the manifold.  Now we'll reveal a secret:  if we "go small enough" we can approximate that the manifold is Euclidean:

But don't get too excited about this revelation.  It only works "locally near a set of points" which means if we move up to say the upper left corner of that grid above and then go "local" the Euclidean grid we observe might be tilted with respect to the one pictured above.  All of this is imaginable in three dimensions - much harder in four or more.

The second type of special line is the orange line and its map pictured below:

That orange line is a path, sometimes called a trajectory, or maybe a worldline.   These are single continuous lines that mathematicians usually call curves, so we are going to call them curve-paths.  Our curve-paths will usually be labeled with a lower-case gamma.  The one depicted above is a map from the real numbers to the coordinates: $\gamma |\;\mathbb{R}\mapsto M\;{\text{as}}\;\gamma \left( s \right)\mapsto \left\{ {x\left( s \right),y\left( s \right)} \right\}$.  Here are the parametric equations for that parabola we added to our picture:
\begin{equation}x\left( s \right) = s + {x_o}\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;{\text{and}}\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;y\left( s \right) = s\left( {s + 2{x_o}} \right) + {y_o}\notag\end{equation}

Notice that ${x_o}$ and ${y_o}$ are the values of the coordinates for the parameter value $s = 0$.  In the image we have set ${x_o} = 1$ and ${y_o} = 1$, so $s = 0$ is not the origin of our coordinate system.

The third type of special line is the set of black circles and one of their maps pictured below:

The black, concentric circles are contours of what we call function-fields.  These are families of lines that connect the points in the manifold whose coordinates produce that same value for the function. A function is map from the manifold to the real numbers, for instance $f|\;M\mapsto \mathbb{R}\;{\text{as}}\;f\left( {x,y} \right) = r\text{; }r \in \mathbb{R}$.  Pick an $r$ then find all the points $\left( {x,y} \right)$ whose coordinates when inserted into $f\left( {x,y} \right)$ produce the value $r$ and connect them with a line.  Pick another $r$, draw its line, and you'll create a contour system for the function-field.
