1.4.5 The (Inner) Product

Recall the algebraic LLA of the composition map of a function map and a curve map at a point in the manifold model produced the following expression for the directional derivative: \begin{equation}\vec V\cdot \vec \nabla f = {\left( {\frac{{dx}}{{ds}},\frac{{dy}}{{ds}}} \right)_o} \cdot {\left( {\frac{{\partial f}}{{\partial x}},\frac{{\partial f}}{{\partial y}}} \right)_o}\label{Q}\end{equation}

For our ongoing example at $\left( {{x_o},{y_o}} \right) = \left( {1,1} \right)$ where $s = 0$ we found

\begin{equation}{\left( {\frac{{dx}}{{ds}}} \right)_o} = 1\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;{\text{and}}\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;{\left( {\frac{{dy}}{{ds}}} \right)_o} = 2\notag\end{equation} \begin{equation}{\left( {\frac{{\partial f}}{{\partial x}}} \right)_{\left( {1,1} \right)}} = {\left( {2x} \right)_{\left( {1,1} \right)}} = 2\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;{\text{and}}\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;{\left( {\frac{{\partial f}}{{\partial y}}} \right)_{\left( {1,1} \right)}} = {\left( {2y} \right)_{\left( {1,1} \right)}} = 2\notag\end{equation} Then the scalar product in \eqref{Q} evaluates to \begin{equation}\vec V\cdot \vec \nabla f = \left( {1,2} \right) \cdot \left( {2,2} \right) = 2 + 4 = 6\notag\end{equation} How do we see this graphically in our magnified coordinates in the associated tangent and cotangent spaces?  The graphical LLA of the curve-path map produced the tangent vector \begin{equation}\vec V = {\left( {\frac{{dx}}{{ds}},\frac{{dy}}{{ds}}} \right)_o}\notag\end{equation} shown as a red arrow in tangent space of Fig 1.4h and Fig 1.4i.  Our graphical LLA of the function-field map, formerly called the gradient vector \begin{equation}\vec \nabla f = {\left( {\frac{{\partial f}}{{\partial x}},\frac{{\partial f}}{{\partial y}}} \right)_o},\notag\end{equation} is now represented in our cotangent space figure by a set of (blue) fixed space parallel lines, tangent to the associated contour line in the manifold “below” it. A first glance seems to strongly suggest that the blue parallel line system is operating on the red arrow in that one can "see" the arrow cross about six of the blue lines.  If we decompose the vector in directions perpendicular to and parallel to the cotangent lines, shown in yellow below, we see the perpendicular yellow component spans 6 spacings, while the yellow component parallel to the blue lines spans none of them.  


So ignoring the parallel yellow segment we can tentatively conclude that the blue cotangent lines “measure” the perpendicular vector segment below it with the result of about 6.  We can improve our confidence that the result is exactly 6 with a little trigonometry.

The spacing between those blue lines, which are oriented at ${45^{\text{o}}}$ to the grid below it, is one side of a right isosceles triangle whose hypotenuse is 1/2 giving us a value of $1/\left( {2\sqrt 2 } \right) \cong 0.354$.


The component of the vector perpendicular to the blue lines is one side of another right triangle whose hypotenuse is the magnitude of the vector $\left\{ {1,2} \right\}$ which is $\sqrt 5 $.


We can compute the length of the component as $\sqrt 5 \cos \left( {{\text{atan}}\left( 2 \right) - {\text{atan}}\left( 1 \right)} \right) = \sqrt 5 \cos \left( {{{18.4}^{\text{o}}}} \right) = 2.121$.  Finally we divide this by the distance between blue lines to find:  $2.121/0.354 = 5.992 \to 6$.  In the next section we will see how this result is achieved much more naturally.

A fanciful way of describing this “measurement” of a vector by a covector is suggested by the symbol used for covectors (1-forms) in such diagrams.


The little carrot symbol shows the direction of measurement – in this case the direction of function’s greatest increase in magnitude – and it moves forward in equally spaced jumps, “consuming” the underlying vector.  For those old enough to remember video games of yore, it behaves like a pac-man eating things that get in the way.  With the red tangent vector fixed the little parallel line segment jumps in parallel steps "up" the red arrow reporting back how many steps were parallel to the line segment spacing.  The three figures above would represent a partial measurement of "3".

The process of measurement described above involving cotangents operating on tangents (or vice versa, depending on your point of view) that results in a scalar (number) is called a contraction.  In stark contrast to results of a traditional scalar dot product of vectors, which produces zero if vectors are orthogonal, the contraction operation only produces non-zero answers only when a component is orthogonal (in the sense of our construction using parallel lines to represent the covector).  This is because the contraction is an operation between different dual objects – a vector and a covector – rather than between similar objects in the scalar dot product operation of two vectors.

Symbolically the contraction operation replaces \begin{equation}\vec V\cdot \vec \nabla f\notag\end{equation} with \begin{equation}\left\langle {V,df} \right\rangle \notag\end{equation} Spaces whose elements appear in contraction operation are called dual spaces. We will use the contraction operation repeatedly in coming chapters.
