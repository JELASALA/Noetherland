3.4 Isovectors of PDE Systems

3.4.2. Contact Manifold

Suhubi’s process provides a more systematic approach to encoding the PDE system into differential forms, following by applying the Lie derivative to the collection of such forms.  It begins by considering what are called “contact” 1-forms and the space they inhabit.  There is a vast history behind this concept but we will try to boil it down to relevancy. 


Back in the Chapter 1 we introduced various “spaces” used by authors and alluded to what Suhubi calls ${m^{th}}$-order contact space and what Olver calls a jet bundle, which are expansions of Edelen's Kinematic space to include all derivatives of the dependent variable(s) represented by a proliferation of manifold “independent” variables, referred to as ${C_m}$ (see diagram).

We pass from the base manifold ${\mathcal{D}_n}$ to ${C_m}$ via the intermediate Graph space $\mathcal{G}$ that contains independent and dependent variable(s).  We have simplified and re-labeled the graph from the introduction to conform more or less to Suhubi’s notation.  One significant difference from the introductory graph [GRAPHNUMBER] is the use of the $\left\{ {{x^i}} \right\}$ all the way up, rather than potential mappings of the independent coordinates, something Suhubi justifies in section 9.2.  We have redundantly labeled the upper space as Kinematic space $K\left\{ {{x^i},{u^\alpha },v_j^\alpha } \right\}$ and Contact space ${C_m}$ explained below.  Note superscripts in Greek indices run from 1 to N, numbering all the possible dependent variables in a system of PDEs.  When absent, we are considering only a single dependent variable.

The problem at hand is we are given a PDE system of the form:

\begin{equation}{F^a}\left( {{x^i},{u^\alpha },u_{,{i_1}}^\alpha ,u_{,{i_1}{i_2}}^\alpha , \ldots ,u_{,{i_1}{i_2} \ldots {i_m}}^\alpha } \right) = 0\end{equation}

where m is the order of the PDE, $a = 1, \ldots ,A,\;\;\;i = 1, \ldots ,n\;\;{\text{and}}\;\;\alpha  = 1, \ldots ,N$ for a PDE system of A equations, n independent variables and N dependent variables.  The presence of the multi-index in the general PDE system representation will remind you of Olver’s prolongation process, a timely reminder these “technologies” are addressing the same problem.

Our $A = 1$ heat equation example earlier would be written as:

\begin{equation}0 = {u_{xx}} - {u_t} = F\left( {x,t,{u_t},{u_{xx}}} \right) \to F\left( {{x^1},{x^2},{u_{{x^2}}},{u_{{x^1}{x^1}}}} \right) = 0\notag\end{equation} where $m = 2,\;n = 2,\;N = 1$. 

Suhubi defines a mapping $\Phi $ that maps u to $\phi $ in Graph space $\mathcal{G}$, but as there is no functional change in the coordinates used to define $\Phi $ (in general there is) we can simply write $\phi \left( {{x^i}} \right) = u\left( {{x^i}} \right)$ there.  Now Graph space does not accommodate derivatives as independent variables.  We need to map these other PDE variables to appropriate coordinates in the overlying Contact manifold or ${C_m}$ space (an extension of Edelen’s Kinematic space).  We demand $\Phi $ take us up there as well.  It turns out the easiest way to infer $\Phi $ is by defining contact forms in the ${T^*}\left( {{C_m}} \right)$ space of ${C_m}$ and demanding that pulling them back to base space ${\mathcal{D}_n}$ with ${\Phi ^*}$ makes the correct connections. 

We propose to replace all derivatives of the dependent variable(s) with auxiliary variables that we may treat as independent variables in the very large Contact manifold.  As a system of first order derivatives Kinematic space suffices, where the first derivatives of the dependent variable are encoded in a new “independent” variable, shown as $v_i^\alpha $ in the figure.  But in general we will need to encode higher derivatives as first order derivatives of variables one order smaller.  Suhubi does this in his (9.2.6) as follows:

\begin{equation}{F^a}\left( {{x^i},{u^\alpha },v_{{i_1}}^\alpha , \ldots ,v_{{i_1}{i_2} \ldots {i_m}}^\alpha } \right) = 0\label{S926}\end{equation}

by assigning

\begin{equation}u_{,{i_1}{i_2} \ldots {i_r}}^\alpha  = v_{{i_1}{i_2} \ldots {i_r}}^\alpha  = v_{{i_1}{i_2} \ldots {i_{r - 1}}{i_r}}^\alpha \label{S924}\end{equation}

Note indices range as follows:  $0 \leqslant r \leqslant m$, $1 \leqslant {i_1}, \ldots ,{i_r} \leqslant n$ and by convention $v_{{i_0}}^\alpha  \equiv {u^\alpha }$.  Suhubi points out that this process converts an ${m^{th}}$-order PDE in base-space into a FOPDE algebraic system defining a sub-manifold in the contact manifold ${C_m}$ or K-space.

Now we use ${u^\alpha }$ for the dependent variable(s) and a set of new, auxiliary variables to represent its derivatives.  Note the absence of commas in the subscripts.  A comma generally indicates derivatives follow, indexed by various Latin indices.  Now those indices, sans commas, simply distinguish different “independent” variables from each other.  The space in which all the variables in the argument list for $F$ live in is Suhubi’s ${m^{th}}$-order contact space (aka Olver’s jet space) – a space of dimension $n + N\frac{{\left( {n + m} \right)!}}{{n!m!}}$, a number that grows rapidly with order and base dimension.  For the 1D heat equation examined earlier we have $N = 1,\;n = 2,\;m = 2$, yielding an 8 dimensional contact manifold:  $\left\{ {x,t,u,{u_x},{u_t},{u_{xx}},{u_{xt}},{u_{tt}}} \right\}$ becoming $\left\{ {{x^1}{x^2},u,{v_1},{v_2},{v_{11}},{v_{12}},{v_{22}}} \right\}$.  Of course not all of this space is “used” by the heat equation example.

Suhubi concludes that \eqref{S926} represents an encoding of the PDE systems as a set of algebraic equations in the contact manifold, and since he retains the notion that \eqref{S924} implies a 1st order set of differential equations, the problem is now first order.  We see directly the need to maintain first order derivatives in the definition of the contact 1-forms, as follows.

Through great insight Cartan found the following “works,” though it is may not be the only way to accomplish this feat.  This entails defining contact 1-forms and demanding their pullback mapping generate the appropriate derivatives in the base space.  Suhubi makes an interesting comment on rationale for using 1-forms:

“In order to study a system of partial differential equations via exterior forms we have to [greatly] enlarge this system to that of first order partial differential equations by introducing auxiliary variables because of the fact that only the first order exterior derivatives are not identically nil.” (p. 489).

Proceeding, we define contact 1-forms:

\begin{equation}\sigma _{{i_1}{i_2} \ldots {i_r}}^\alpha  = dv_{{i_1}{i_2} \ldots {i_r}}^\alpha  - v_{{i_1}{i_2} \ldots {i_r},i}^\alpha d{x^i},\;\;0 \leqslant r \leqslant m - 1\end{equation}

and by convention

\begin{equation}\sigma _{{i_0}}^\alpha  \equiv {\sigma ^\alpha } = d{u^\alpha } - v_i^\alpha d{x^i},\;{\text{for}}\;r = 0\end{equation}

For example, for the 1D heat equation $m = 2,\;n = 2$ so

\begin{equation}{\sigma ^\alpha } = d{u^\alpha } - v_i^\alpha d{x^i}\;\;\;\notag\end{equation}\begin{equation}\sigma _1^\alpha  = dv_1^\alpha  - v_{1,i}^\alpha d{x^i}\;\;\notag\end{equation}\begin{equation}\sigma _2^\alpha  = dv_2^\alpha  - v_{2,i}^\alpha d{x^i}\;\;\notag\end{equation} The proposition is that pulling back such contact 1-forms causes them to vanish, the utility of which will be extended to an ideal formed by them that represents the PDE we are encoding.  Let us demonstrate.

We pull back the general contact form with ${\phi ^*}$.  We will use the properties of the pullback operation described in Section  1.7 (based on Pushing and pulling between mappings-Final2.docx):

\begin{equation}{\phi ^*}f = f \circ \phi \;\;{\text{[inverion of}}\;f\left( {{x^i}} \right) \to \bar f\left( {{u^i}}\right){\text{]}} \notag\end{equation}

\begin{equation}{\phi ^*}\left( {\alpha  + \beta } \right) = {\phi ^*}\alpha  + {\phi ^*}\beta \notag\end{equation}\begin{equation}{\phi ^*}\left( {\alpha  \wedge \beta } \right) = {\phi ^*}\alpha  \wedge {\phi ^*}\beta \notag\end{equation}\begin{equation}{\phi ^*}\left( {d\alpha } \right) = d\left( {{\phi ^*}\alpha } \right)\notag\end{equation} Start with the $r = 0$ equation: \begin{equation}{\phi ^*}{\sigma ^\alpha } = {\phi ^*}d{u^\alpha } - {\phi ^*}\left( {v_i^\alpha d{x^i}} \right)\notag\end{equation} The first term becomes, via property (d):

\begin{equation}{\phi ^*}d{u^\alpha } = d\left( {{\phi ^*}{u^\alpha }} \right) = d{u^\alpha }\notag\end{equation} by design, as $\phi \left( {{x^i}} \right) = u\left( {{x^i}} \right)$.  The second term is found using properties (a) and (c):

\begin{equation}{\phi ^*}\left( {v_i^\alpha d{x^i}} \right) = \left( {{\phi ^*}v_i^\alpha } \right)\left( {{\phi ^*}d{x^i}} \right) = \left( {{\phi ^*}v_i^\alpha } \right)d{x^i}\notag\end{equation} since ${\phi ^*}d{x^i} = d{x^i}$ by design.  Now

\begin{equation}{\phi ^*}v_i^\alpha  = u_{,i}^\alpha \notag\end{equation}So by the chain rule the pull-back of ${\sigma ^\alpha }$ yields \begin{equation}{\phi ^*}{\sigma ^\alpha } = d{u^\alpha } - u_{,i}^\alpha d{x^i} = d{u^\alpha } - \frac{{\partial {u^\alpha }}}{{\partial {x^i}}}d{x^i} = 0\;\;\emptyset \notag\end{equation}Next level $r = 1$: \begin{equation}{\phi ^*}\sigma _{{i_1}}^\alpha  = {\phi ^*}dv_{{i_1}}^\alpha  - {\phi ^*}\left( {v_{{i_1},i}^\alpha d{x^i}} \right)\notag\end{equation} \begin{equation}{\phi ^*}\sigma _{{i_1}}^\alpha  = d\left( {{\phi ^*}v_{{i_1}}^\alpha } \right) - \left( {{\phi ^*}v_{{i_1},i}^\alpha } \right)d{x^i}\notag\end{equation} \begin{equation}{\phi ^*}\sigma _{{i_1}}^\alpha  = d\left( {u_{,{i_1}}^\alpha } \right) - u_{,{i_1}i}^\alpha d{x^i}\notag\end{equation} \begin{equation}{\phi ^*}\sigma _{{i_1}}^\alpha  = du_{,{i_1}}^\alpha  - \frac{{\partial u_{,{i_1}}^\alpha }}{{\partial {x^i}}}d{x^i} = 0\;\;\emptyset \notag\end{equation} which is just the chain rule at the next level.  In general, the pull back produces:

\begin{equation}{\phi ^*}\sigma _{{i_1}{i_2} \ldots {i_r}}^\alpha  = du_{,{i_1}{i_2} \ldots {i_r}}^\alpha  - \frac{{\partial u_{,{i_1}{i_2} \ldots {i_r}}^\alpha }}{{\partial {x^i}}}d{x^i} = 0;\;\;\;0 \leqslant r \leqslant m - 1\notag\end{equation} So these contact 1-forms embody the correct identification (via a pullback) of the contact manifold “coordinates” with the appropriate partial derivatives of the dependent variable(s).  The other requirement is that the pull-back of the PDE function(s) must also vanish, turning the algebraic relationship among K-space coordinates defined by ${F^a}\left( {{x^i},{u^\alpha },v_{{i_1}}^\alpha , \ldots ,v_{{i_1}{i_2} \ldots {i_m}}^\alpha } \right) = 0$  back to those of the original PDE. \begin{equation}{\phi ^*}{F^a} = {F^a}\left( {{x^i},{u^\alpha },u_{,{i_1}}^\alpha , \ldots ,u_{,{i_1}{i_2} \ldots {i_m}}^\alpha } \right) = 0\notag\end{equation} In reflection, we showed that the variables and their relationships in the contact manifold are correct representations of the original problem in base space.  In reality the identification of the mapping function $\Phi $ and its pull-back to the original PDE problem seems unremarkable and just a simple matter of definitions; indeed we never explicitly write out that mapping but only its effect on the variables of the problem.  But its real value is to confirm we have the “right” EDS representation of the problem as cast in the ${T^*}\left( {{C_m}} \right)$ space.
