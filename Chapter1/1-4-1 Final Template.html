1.4.1 Motivation

In the previous section we found an “invariant” solution

\begin{equation}\zeta \left( {x,y} \right) = {x^2} + {y^2} \equiv {r^2}\notag\end{equation}
to this partial differential equation: \begin{equation}{V_R}\cdot \zeta \left( {x,y} \right) =  - y\frac{{\partial \zeta }}{{\partial x}} + x\frac{{\partial \zeta }}{{\partial y}} = 0\notag\end{equation}

resulting from application of the rotation symmetry transformation to such a function and demanding it vanish.  This produces circles of radius $r$ about the origin.  As we move along such a circle visiting points $\left( {x,y} \right)$ we are following an infinite number of “orbits” of the invariance family of this transformation.  Plotting these orbits produces a system of contours of the field-function $\zeta \left( {x,y} \right) = {x^2} + {y^2}$:


Moving along a contour, while satisfying from an invariance standpoint, is rather boring, simply sampling one of many invariance orbits that solves the PDE invariance condition.  We could locally explore the field, and discover some interesting new mathematics, if we depart from a contour and move along an independent curve-path that intersects contours.  That is, we seek to explore the interaction of curve-paths and function-fields.  Mathematically that exploration begins by forming a composition of curve-paths and function-fields.

In the following sub-sections we will explore the interaction of our curve-path and field-function examples (as representative of the general case) from the standpoint of an observer (say, Inchie) on a curve-path sampling a field as it moves through (and across) contour lines of that field-function.

 [The material presented in this Section was motivated by the discussions in William Burke's 1980 text Spacetme, Geometry, and Cosmology Chapter Two - Geometry, University Science Books.]
