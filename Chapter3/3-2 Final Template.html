3.2. The Ideal and Characteristic Vectors

Ideal. This brings us to the concept of the ideal. Symbolically we can represent an ideal $I\left\{ {{\alpha _1},{\alpha _2}, \ldots ,{\alpha _k}} \right\}$ as follows: \begin{equation}\gamma \wedge {\alpha _i} \in \;I\end{equation} where $I\left\{ {{\alpha _1},{\alpha _2}, \ldots ,{\alpha _k}} \right\}$ is a subset of a larger “graded” space $\Lambda \left( M \right)$ and where $\gamma$ is any differential form in $\Lambda \left( M \right)$. The ${\alpha _i}$ are referred to as generators of the ideal. According to Edelen: “an ideal I is said to be generated by ${\alpha _1},{\alpha _2}, \ldots ,{\alpha _k}$ iff every element of I is the sum of terms, each of which contains at least one of the elements ${\alpha _i}$ as a factor.” For example (Edelen p. 105), in $M = {E_4}$ let \begin{equation}{\alpha _1} = 2dx - 3ydz\notag\end{equation}\begin{equation}{\alpha _2} = xdy - zdt\notag\end{equation}\begin{equation}{\alpha _3} = {x^2}tdx \wedge dt - tdy \wedge dz\notag\end{equation} Then any 1-form in $I\left\{ {{\alpha _1},{\alpha _2},{\alpha _3}} \right\}$ must look like \begin{equation}\omega \left( 1 \right) = f{\alpha _1} + g{\alpha _2}\notag\end{equation}and any 2-form must look like \begin{equation}\sigma \left( 2 \right) = \beta \left( 1 \right) \wedge {\alpha _1} + \gamma \left( 1 \right) \wedge {\alpha _2} + h{\alpha _3}\notag\end{equation} where I have used parentheses to indicate the degree of the form where it may be ambiguous. Note there is nothing magic here; this is simply a definition. Arbitrary forms $\gamma $ in $\Lambda \left( M \right)$ are not “in the club” by themselves. A specialized ideal is the closed ideal, which we will address further below.

Characteristic Vectors. Before introducing closed ideals we will discuss the dual vector space to an ideal generated by 1-forms, called the characteristic subspace, containing the characteristic vectors of that ideal. They are defined by the contraction: \begin{equation}\left\langle {{V_j},{\omega ^i}} \right\rangle = 0;i = 1, \ldots ,r;\;j = r + 1, \ldots ,m;{\omega ^i} \in {\Lambda ^1}\left( M \right)\end{equation} where the r different ${\omega ^i}$ generate the ideal and the ${V_j}$ are the characteristic vectors for the manifold of dimension m. Note the requirement that the number of 1-forms plus the number of characteristic vectors totals to the dimension of the manifold holds only for ideals generated by 1-forms.

From Suhubi Example 5.6.2, consider an ideal in $M = {E_4}$ generated by \begin{equation}{\omega ^1} = dx - ydz\notag\end{equation}\begin{equation}{\omega ^2} = dx - xdy + tdz\notag\end{equation} Then a characteristic vector of that ideal \begin{equation}V = {v^x}{\partial _x} + {v^y}{\partial _y} + {v^z}{\partial _z} + {v^t}{\partial _t}\notag\end{equation}must satisfy \begin{equation}\left\langle {V,{\omega ^i}} \right\rangle = 0,\;i = 1,2\notag\end{equation}This generates two equations: \begin{equation}{v^x} - y{v^z} = 0\notag\end{equation}\begin{equation}{v^x} - x{v^y} + t{v^z} = 0\notag\end{equation}Together these imply \begin{equation}{v^x} = y{v^z}\notag\end{equation}\begin{equation}{v^y} = \frac{{y + t}}{x}{v^z}\notag\end{equation} We can choose ${v^z} = 1$, yielding the first characteristic vector: \begin{equation}{V_1} = y{\partial _x} + \frac{{y + t}}{x}{\partial _y} + {\partial _z}\notag\end{equation} The second vector (there must be $4 - 2 = 2$) is found by realizing there were no conditions on ${v^t}$, so we can set it to 1 yielding \begin{equation}{V_2} = {\partial _t}\;\;\color{blue}\blacksquare \notag\end{equation} An extension of this idea applies to ideal generated by forms of diverse degrees. Then the condition for characteristic vectors is the existence of $\lambda _b^a$ — forms in $\Lambda \left( M \right)$ of suitable degrees — such that: \begin{equation}\left\langle {{V_j},{\omega ^a}} \right\rangle = \lambda _b^a \wedge {\omega ^b},a,b = 1, \ldots ,r;\;j = r + 1, \ldots ,m;\;{\omega ^a} \in \Lambda \left( M \right);\;\lambda _b^a \in \Lambda \left( M \right)\end{equation}
Note: the RHS of this relation produces forms that, by definition, are in the ideal we started with, since for an ideal $I\left\{ {{\omega ^1}, \ldots ,{\omega ^r}} \right\}$ its members satisfy $\gamma \wedge {\omega ^a} \in \;I$ for $\gamma \in \Lambda \left( M \right)$.

In Suhubi Example 5.6.3 (and Edelen page 108), consider an ideal $I\left\{ {{\omega ^1},{\omega ^2}} \right\}$ in $M = {E_4}$ generated by \begin{equation}{\omega ^1} = dx - ydz\notag\end{equation}\begin{equation}{\omega ^2} = tdx \wedge dz - xdy \wedge dt\notag\end{equation} Then a vector \begin{equation}V = {v^x}{\partial _x} + {v^y}{\partial _y} + {v^z}{\partial _z} + {v^t}{\partial _t}\notag\end{equation}must satisfy \begin{equation}\left\langle {V,{\omega ^1}} \right\rangle = 0\notag\end{equation} as contraction reduces the degree of the form by one and there are no zero-forms in the ideal, and \begin{equation}\left\langle {V,{\omega ^2}} \right\rangle = \lambda {\omega ^1}\notag\end{equation}for $\lambda $ a function. These conditions lead to: \begin{equation}{v^x} - y{v^z} = 0\notag\end{equation}and \begin{equation}t{v^x}dz - t{v^z}dx - x{v^y}dt + x{v^t}dy = \lambda dx - \lambda ydz\notag\end{equation}So \begin{equation} - t{v^z} = \lambda \notag\end{equation}\begin{equation}t{v^x} = - \lambda y\notag\end{equation}\begin{equation} - x{v^y} = 0\notag\end{equation}\begin{equation}x{v^t} = 0\notag\end{equation}Solving the first two together gives us: \begin{equation}{v^z}/{v^x} = 1/y\notag\end{equation} We can choose, say, ${v^z} = 1$ so ${v^x} = y$, giving us \begin{equation}V = y{\partial _x} + {\partial _y}\;\color{blue}\blacksquare \notag\end{equation} Now we might expect there to be another characteristic vector of the ideal since $m = 4$ and $r = 2$. As Edelen points out, for ideals generated by anything but 1-forms, we do not reach such a conclusion. Suhubi’s version of this example includes three more “characteristic vectors” of the generator forms themselves which seem not relevant if we are focusing on the ideal.

Closed Ideal. A closed ideal satisfies $d\omega \in I$ for all $\omega \in I$. What could be simpler? We can start with an arbitrary ideal and close it by extending the list of its generators. For example, if \begin{equation}I\left\{ {{\omega _1}, \ldots ,{\omega _r}} \right\}\notag\end{equation}then we can provisionally add to it: \begin{equation}I\left\{ {{\omega _1}, \ldots ,{\omega _r},d{\omega _1}, \ldots ,d{\omega _r}} \right\}\notag\end{equation} Now it may be that one or more of the exterior derivatives generate a redundant member (a linear combination some of the ${\omega _i}$), in which case we remove that exterior derivative from the list of generators.

Suhubi Example 5.10.1. Return to the ideal generated by \begin{equation}{\omega ^1} = dx - ydz\notag\end{equation}\begin{equation}{\omega ^2} = tdx \wedge dz - xdy \wedge dt\notag\end{equation}Now \begin{equation}d{\omega ^1} = - dy \wedge dz\notag\end{equation} Can $d{\omega ^1}$ be written as a linear combination of basis forms? \begin{equation}d{\omega ^1} = - dy \wedge dz = ? = \Gamma _1^1 \wedge \left( {dx - ydz} \right) + \Gamma _1^2 \wedge \left( {tdx \wedge dz - xdy \wedge dt} \right)\notag\end{equation} where the Gammas are forms of appropriate degree. In this case $\Gamma _1^1$ must be a 1-form and $\Gamma _1^2$ a zero-form (function). To check we assume a general form $\Gamma _1^1 = gdx + hdy + pdz + qdt$, substitute it in and match coefficients of the (independent) basis 1-forms. A little algebra tells us that there are no self-consistent solutions for the coefficients of $\Gamma _1^1$ and the function $\Gamma _1^2$, so $d{\omega ^1}$ is an independent member of the ideal. What about $d{\omega ^2}$? \begin{equation}d{\omega ^2} = dt \wedge dx \wedge dz - dx \wedge dy \wedge dt = \left( {\frac{1}{x}dx + \frac{1}{t}dt} \right) \wedge {\omega ^2}\notag\end{equation} so this is redundant. The full generator list for this closed ideal is$\;I\left\{ {{\omega ^1},{\omega ^2},dy \wedge dz} \right\}\;\color{blue}\blacksquare $.
