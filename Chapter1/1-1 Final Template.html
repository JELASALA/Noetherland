A common theme – and the underlying theme of this e-tutorial – is the intimate relationship between symmetry, invariance and conservation.  We will focus initially on the first two concepts, which are features of group theory, specifically Lie group transformations and their properties.

Definitions

INVARIANCE:  when an expression of a mathematical theory or a physically observable behavior does not vary under the conditions of a mathematical and/or physical change.
Operationally: we have to impose change to discover things that don't change.

SYMMETRY:  when a mathematical transformation applied to such an expression preserves its essential properties we have identified a symmetry of that expression. That is, the expression obeying a symmetry operation is left invariant under such transformation operation.
Operationally: we use imposition of change to find these special transformations if they exist.  These transformation equations usually form a group.

CONSERVATION: [PROVISIONAL] when invariance exists and its symmetry group has been identified, often one finds specific quantities that are truly constant under operation of the expression representing underlying dynamics.
Operationally:  invariance and symmetry can provide some special, valuable quantities for both theory, experiment, and application that are accountable (???).

Origins. In the mid-19th century mathematicians realized that assumptions that models utilizing only three dimensions were inadequate.  Newton's Second Law, for instance, demanded three just for space and a fourth for time.  Expanding dimensionality of phenomena was just one of an enormous explosion of new mathematics that would couple with the new discoveries in all of science for the next 200 years.

The modern model of what physicists call a "configuration space" is called a manifold.  The simplest definition of a manifold is a continuous set of points each of which has coordinates, often more than three.  Spacetime of Special Relativity uses $\left( {t,x,y,z} \right)$, thermodynamics often uses $\left( {U,\;P,V,T,S} \right)$ and sometimes more.
